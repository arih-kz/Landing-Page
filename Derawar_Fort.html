The Cholistan Desert, part of the western Thar Desert, shows evidence of Indus Valley culture that once used the Hakra River for agriculture. Derawar Fort is the best-preserved structure of several ancient forts in the area.

Built in the 9th century AD by Rai Jajja Bhati, a Hindu Rajput ruler, the fort was originally named Dera Rawal and later renamed Derawar. In the 18th century, the fort was taken over by the Nawabs of Bahawalpur, renovated in 1732 by Nawab Sadeq Muhammad, and briefly lost and regained by Nawab Mubarak Khan. The fort was later used by the British to house prisoners. Google Doodle celebrated Derawar Fort on Pakistan's Independence Day, symbolizing Pakistani adaptability and antiquity.

Derawar Fort is a large square structure built of clay bricks, with a length of 1500 meters and a height of up to 30 meters. It features forty circular bastions, ten on each side, each intricately decorated. The interior includes tile and fresco artwork.

Despite these efforts, the site faces deterioration due to severe weather and neglect.

Access to Derawar requires permission from the privately owned Abbasi Family. It is visited annually by over 70,000 people during the Cholistan Desert jeep rally. The fort was submitted for consideration as a World Heritage Site in 2016 for its significance in ancient trade routes and architectural adaptation to the desert environment.
