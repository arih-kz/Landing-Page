Baltit Fort, the former residence of the Mirs of Hunza, is a significant landmark. Before the arrival of Islam, Buddhism was the primary religion, and the region holds several surviving Buddhist archaeological sites, such as the Sacred Rock of Hunza. Hunza was central in the network of trading routes connecting Central Asia to the subcontinent, playing a role in the transmission of Buddhism throughout Asia.

Since the arrival of Islam, most of the population has converted to the Ismaili sect. The region has ancient Brahmi script graffiti on rocks, produced by Buddhist monks, now being restored. Hunza was an independent principality for more than 900 years, captured by the British Empire in 1891. The ruler of Hunza, Mir Safdar Ali Khan, fled to Kashgar, China, and the British installed his brother Mir Nazim Khan as a puppet ruler.

The Hunza Valley is home to several high peaks above 7,000 meters, including Rakaposhi, Distaghil Sar, Batura, and others. The valley provides views of several mountains, including Ultar Sar, Bojahagur Duanasir II, and Ladyfinger Peak. Hunza has a diverse religious history with historic sites like the ancient watchtowers in Ganish village, Baltit Fort, and Altit Fort. Prehistoric carvings of men and animals are found on rocks along the valley.

Hunza also houses several lakes, including Attabad Lake, Borith Lake, and Shimshal Lakes. The 57 km long Batura Glacier, the fifth-longest glacier in the world outside the polar region, is surrounded by Shispare, Batura, and Kumpirdior peaks. The largest mountain pass, Khunjerab Pass, is 4,693 meters high and strategically positioned on the northern border of Pakistan and the southwest border of China.

Hiking treks include Ondra Poygah Gulmit and Leopard Trek Shiskhat. The valley is believed to be one of the inspirations for Shangri-La in James Hilton's novel Lost Horizon. The local languages spoken include Burushaski, Wakhi, and Shina. The literacy rate of the Hunza Valley is over 95%, with a strong emphasis on education, especially for girls, influenced by the Aga Khan's contributions.

The historical area of Hunza has seen migrations, conflicts, and resettling of tribes, with the Shina people being prominent. The Hunza Valley is also home to some Wakhi, who migrated from northeastern Afghanistan in the nineteenth century. The majority of residents in Hunza belong to the Ismaili Shia sect of Islam, with Aga Khan IV as their religious figurehead.

Hunza's longevity has been noted, though some refute this as a myth due to the lack of birth records. While evidence of extraordinary longevity is lacking, the Hunza people lead a healthy lifestyle, with a diet rich in apricot seeds and oils, and glacier water rich in minerals. The landscape encourages high levels of exercise, contributing to their overall health.
