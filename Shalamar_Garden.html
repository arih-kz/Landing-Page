The gardens' design was influenced by the older Shalimar Gardens in Kashmir, built by Shah Jahan's father, Emperor Jahangir. Unlike the Kashmir gardens, which utilized natural slopes, the Lahore waterworks required extensive engineering for artificial cascades and terraces. The site was chosen for its stable water supply. The project was managed by Khalilullah Khan and Mulla Alaul Maulk Tuni, with Ali Mardan Khan responsible for the construction, including a 100-mile-long canal to bring water from Kashmir. The Shalimar Gardens originally belonged to the Arain Mian family of Baghbanpura. Mian Muhammad Yusuf, head of the family, ceded the site to Shah Jahan. In return, the family governed the gardens for over 350 years.

Construction began on 12 June 1641 and took 18 months to complete. During the Sikh era, much of the garden's marble was pillaged for the Golden Temple and the Ram Bagh Palace in Amritsar. The gardens' agate gate was stripped and sold. In 1806, Maharaja Ranjit Singh ordered repairs. The gardens were nationalized in 1962 by General Ayub Khan, who also forbade the Mela Chiraghan festival in 1958.

The Shalimar Gardens are a rectangle aligned along a north–south axis, measuring 658 metres by 258 metres and covering 16 hectares. Each terrace level is 4–5 metres higher than the previous one. The uppermost terrace, named Bagh-e-Farah Baksh, means "Bestower of Pleasure." The second and third terraces are known as Bagh-e-Faiz Baksh, meaning "Bestower of Goodness." The terraces are divided into smaller squares by long fountains, with cascades flowing over marble paths into a large pool, known as a haūz, with a seating pavilion.
